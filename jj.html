Supreme    Court     Tightens    the     Noose  on  Defection  Delays 

ADV. MOHD HAMZA January 3, 2026

Table of Contents

  • Supreme    Court     Tightens    the     Noose  on  Defection  Delays

Introduction

The Tenth Schedule of the Indian Constitution, popularly known as the Anti-Defection Law, was introduced to curb the menace of political floor-crossing that undermined democratic stability. However, a significant loophole has long persisted: the inaction of the Speaker. In a decisive move to plug this gap, the Supreme Court of India, in the recent landmark case of Padi Kaushik Reddy v. State of Telangana, has reaffirmed that the Speaker’s office is not a sanctuary for indefinite delays.

The Controversy

The case arose when petitions were filed seeking the disqualification of certain Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) who had defected from the Bharat Rashtra Samithi (BRS) to the ruling Congress party in Telangana. Despite the clear mandate of the Tenth Schedule, the disqualification petitions remained pending before the Speaker for a prolonged period. The petitioner, Padi Kaushik Reddy, approached the Supreme Court, arguing that the delay amounted to a denial of justice and defeated the very purpose of the anti-defection provisions.

The Supreme Court’s Ruling

A two-judge bench comprising Justice B.R. Gavai and Justice K.V. Viswanathan took a grim view of the delay. Relying on the precedent set in Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. The Hon’ble Speaker Manipur (2020), the Court reiterated that the Speaker, while adjudicating disqualification petitions, functions as a Tribunal. Therefore, their decisions—or lack thereof—are subject to judicial review.

The Bench rejected the contention that the Court cannot intervene until a final order is passed (Quia Timet action). They held that when inaction threatens the constitutional scheme, the Court has the duty to step in. The Supreme Court directed the Speaker of the Telangana Legislative Assembly to decide on the disqualification petitions within a reasonable timeframe, setting a specific deadline to ensure accountability.

Legal Significance

This judgment is a critical evolution in Indian constitutional law for three reasons:

1. Tribunal Status It solidifies the position that the Speaker is a quasi-judicial authority under the Tenth Schedule, stripping away the absolute immunity often claimed under Article 212.
2. End of Pocket Veto It prevents Speakers from using "pocket vetoes"—simply sitting on petitions—to allow defecting members to complete their terms or hold ministerial positions illegitimately.
3. Judicial Activism It demonstrates the Judiciary's willingness to enforce timelines on the Legislature to preserve democratic ethics.

Conclusion

The Padi Kaushik Reddy judgment serves as a stern reminder that constitutional functionaries must rise above partisan politics. By enforcing a timeline for adjudication, the Supreme Court has ensured that the "Aaya Ram, Gaya Ram" culture cannot be sustained through procedural delays. For the legal fraternity and the electorate alike, this is a welcome step toward a more transparent and accountable parliamentary system.
